    \section*{Research Statement}

    \onehalfspacing
    
    The goal of my PhD work is to bring artificial intelligence (AI) to urban landscapes in ways that bolster equity, efficiency, access to information, and privacy by design. I largely work with millions-scale spatiotemporal datasets, both visual and statistical. This includes large-scale dashcam data, Google Street View, open datasets from New York City's public data platform, NYC OpenData, and realtime General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) data feeds from over 16 transit agencies across the world. My goal is to work on projects that only one or two degrees away from impacting city residents; complementing this, I often collaborate with journalists to publish less-technical descriptions of my proejcts, resulting in coverage from The New York Times, The Economist, Gothamist, and the Fox5 NY and NY1 News news channels. 

    During the first year of my PhD, I harnessed 20 million dashcam images collected during 2020 in New York City, working towards the ability to detect the distribution of NYPD police vehicle deployments.     Here are some statements that summarize my research ethos. (1) Urban planning can not be boiled down to a science. 

    There are fundamental tensions in my work that I am always conscious of, and strive to make strides in address. One is the fact that urban planning is fundamentally a practice rooted in design, not science. I address this by producing \textit{data points} for practitioners downstream of my work, rather than all-encompassing and techno-optimist ideas of dashboard cities and digital twins. Secondly, there are serious ethical considerations to be made when working with data that represents real individuals; especially photographs. I am presently working towards addressing this tension with Helen Nissenbaum \& Cornell's Digital Life Initiative in an audit of group privacy in dense street imagery, which is the data medium I most frequently utilize in my research.
